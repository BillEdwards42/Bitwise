Example Article

March 10, 2025 by Bill Edwards

Not too long ago, the idea of machines surpassing human capabilities sounded absurd. Today, that ‘absurdity’ is, in many cases, the reality. From backoffices run by automated systems to factories run by automated robots, automated machines have overtaken tasks once deemed the exclusive domain of skilled professionals. In today’s world, most jobs are still done by humans. The customer support you’re calling for a defect, the software engineer coding the product you’re using right now, the creators of the content you watch. Though done by humans today, that doesn’t mean it’ll still be the case in the future. Technologies have a strong tendency to improve as time goes by, and when they get good enough, they become an integral part of our life without our consent.


The Backroom Revolution

In the mid-20th century, banks, insurance companies, and government agencies operated enormous “backrooms” staffed by clerks who manually typed, sorted, and cross-referenced a sea of paperwork. Early computers—like IBM’s 650 or the UNIVAC I—were bulky beasts costing hundreds of thousands of dollars. Their punch-card systems were prone to jams, data corruption, and constant mechanical breakdowns. Many executives scoffed at the idea that these unreliable monstrosities could outperform a squad of well-trained clerks, especially given that human workers were typically more flexible and cost less than a massive mainframe plus its dedicated crew of technicians.

But over the following decades, exponential improvements in microprocessor speeds, memory storage, and chip design radically changed the landscape. A 1960s IBM mainframe that filled an entire room offered less processing power than a basic smartphone today. By the 1980s, large institutions were already swapping out file cabinets for digital records, drastically reducing the need for manual data entry. Today, data entries and maintenance were mainly done through automated workflows. Large data centers hold servers after servers to store data that companies collect around the globe, which makes the idea of having a human to collect data manually and store it in a cabinet now seems like a joke. The transformation to these automated back office systems seem only natural.


The Internet Revolution

Few inventions have been as transformative—or initially underestimated—as the Internet. Conceived in the 1960s as part of the U.S. Department of Defense’s ARPANET, it was originally a military-academic experiment, designed for secure and resilient data exchange among a small group of researchers. Early critics questioned the practicality of linking computers across vast distances, suggesting it was too expensive, too specialized, and too fraught with technical hurdles to become mainstream.

Over time, however, improvements in networking protocols (like TCP/IP), the rise of the World Wide Web in the early 1990s, and the gradual drop in computing costs turned the Internet into an unprecedented communication superhighway. By 1995, it was estimated that around 16 million people worldwide were using the Internet—a number that exploded to more than 300 million by 2000, and over 5 billion today. What started as a hard-to-access system for government labs became a global lifeline, underpinning everything from commerce and education to entertainment and social connectivity.

Along the way, it decimated industries reliant on older human-driven models—travel agencies, classified ads, and countless middleman services were replaced or reimagined as digital platforms. This technology that was once deemed too “academic” and “niche” has reshaped how we work, learn, and interact, changing our world completely. Today, the idea of borrowing books, mailing letters, even to an extent, going to a movie theater, seems impractical. The transformation to using the internet in our day to day lives seems only natural.


The Mobile revolution

While landline telephones were already fixtures in most households by the late 20th century, mobile phones had a humbler and more skeptical start. Early models were shaped like actual “bricks” with limited battery life, geared primarily toward businesspeople who could shoulder the high cost. Even the first BlackBerry devices in the early 2000s—lauded for secure email—looked clunky and had tiny keyboards, prompting many consumers to question how these miniature communication hubs could ever beat out laptops or desktop computers.

Yet, as network speeds improved, hardware got smaller, and user interfaces became more intuitive, smartphones grew into powerhouses that could check emails, run sophisticated apps, browse the web, and even handle tasks once impossible for a handheld device. Apple’s iPhone launch in 2007 revolutionized the entire mobile industry; within a few years, it displaced older phone models and accelerated the smartphone’s global adoption. By 2011, smartphone sales had already overtaken PC sales worldwide—a decisive signal that these once “impractical” or “business-only” devices had become the primary tool for communications and productivity, once it became good enough. Today, the idea of not owning a mobile phone itself makes you an oddity. The transformation from clunky, costly “bricks” for a few to sleek, indispensable companions for most now seems only natural.


The ongoing AI revolution

All of these technologies started out impractical, costly, and unperformant. But overtime, as innovations, computing power, chip designs manufacturing progress, the technology becomes good enough and more use cases emerge, replacing humans in many areas and creating jobs in more. Now, since the emergence of ChatGPT in 2022, deep learning, or to most, Artificial Intelligence, has become the newest—potentially most transformative wave of—technology. It automates repetitive jobs, augments human work, and does it all with a chat bot. It may not seem like it is ready to fully take jobs from humans yet, but history has shown that with technology, it is only a matter of time that it becomes “good enough”. And when it becomes good enough, it will become our standard, without consent.

What AI is different from prior waves of technology innovation is that, in its purest form, its goal is to outright replace humans, instead of augmenting them. Why hire a human to use AI, when you can just hire an AI to do the job? They currently lack intuition, so must be leveraged by a human being sitting in front of a computer. Will this always be the case? With time, won’t companies develop ways to remove the middleman—the human using the AI—and simply pay for the AI to do the work? Ofcourse, this will take time, and that is exactly the point. Enterprises will have to restructure their companies or even their definition of jobs to work around the AI agents they want to hire, and AI agents need to be built properly by the software companies first. One day, it might not be humans using services on the internet to finish tasks, it might be AI using services on the internet to finish tasks, rendering large swaths of humanity useless. This is not a fairy tale. The newly launched ChatGPT operator already has the ability to use an internet browser and perform tasks—buying tickets, shopping for groceries, making reservations—on the user's behalf. Though not close to human level, these AIs are indeed, as its name, competing for the position of the “operator”. It’s nice to have them doing these little tasks for you, but you will not think that when it's coming for your job. If the above histories tell us anything, it's that once the technology has been created, it will only get better and better overtime.


Accept and adapt

“Wow, we’re utterly fucked” might be a thought that passes through your mind. I hope the understanding that with time, the computing cost will only go down, and there will only be more, not less, innovations, will help you accept the fact that AI really might one day replace your, and most other human’s jobs. What we see now is a mere beginning, and it's scary enough. Who knows. Maybe after twenty or thirty years, most of us will be on UBI (Universal Basic Income) while only some doing very demanding jobs. Does that mean you should stop working now and accept the fact that you’ll be replaced? No! That day is not even close yet. If you are afraid of being replaced by AI, in my opinion, there are four things that you can do.

  1. 1. Become the minds behind the development of AI
    Technology companies are pouring billions and billions of dollars to fight for the ones with great technical expertise. If you are good enough to become one of them, good for you! You’ll be part of the process towards the next age of humanity and get paid handsomely for that. Sadly, this is not the case for most, and probably not for you.

  2. 2. Build a career in areas that will be hard to replaced by AI
    The issue with this option is that how do you decide which areas will be hard to replace? Any prediction entails the chance of being wrong. With that in mind, one choice is to pursue careers in esoteric fields that will take longer for AI to be better than you. Chip designers, Software engineers, Accountants, doctors. Low level positions of these fields might be replaced by AI, but if you can become one of the best, it’ll be a very hard and very long process for AIs to catch up to you! Next, outside of trying to be hard to replace, you can try to be irreplaceable by pursuing certain fields that are sometimes deemed unfit for AI. This is not due to its technical difficulty, but its need for the human factor. Entrepreneurs, Teachers, Lawyers, Psychiatrist, Waiters. These jobs require physical human interaction and offline work, which is hard for AI to do. Also, plumbers, construction workers, vehicle repairers, these also require physical work, but more effort or strength is needed, which protects you from the AI models in the cloud. I say all this with the caveat that humanoids might one day replace these jobs too, but it will take much longer.

  3. 3. Utilize the AI now and accumulate wealth
    Most companies right now are not using AIs as direct employees, though some are already trying. What you can do between the now and the age of full bloom AI is to utilize the current AI to amplify your ability now. Automate some of your workflows, utilize the chatbots to enhance your work, achieve better results than others that are not using the LLMs (Large Language Models, the chatbots). Ideally, this will earn you a raise, promotion, or a better job offer, as your performance will be augmented by AI while others going about their work just the same as before. The companies are not using AIs to do their work now, but you can. This is not to encourage you to spend less time at work since AI can do a good enough job, it is encouraging you to use it to better your performance and differentiate yourself from others. You will be arbitraging the fact that AI has not yet been adopted by the enterprise world, but you had. You have until wide scale adoption of AI agents from the companies and replacing humans instead of hiring humans using AIs.

  4. 4. Invest in the companies that profit from AI adoptions
    Don’t invest in the first big brand technology company name that comes to mind. What we are trying to do here is to hedge against the chance of losing your job to AIs. What will it mean when that happens? AI agents will become good enough and cost effective enough for companies to start firing employees and replacing them with AIs. They won’t do this because they love technology, but because from a cost analysis standpoint, they will be saving a lot of money and still get the work done. They won’t need to pay for salaries, employee insurances, or office rent. What they’ll need to pay for is the fees for running the AI, possibly to an AI service provider, or the cost of constructing a data center and service themselves. So, in order to hedge against this, we need to also profit from this transformation. Think about companies that will benefit from deploying AI agents as their workforce, the companies that will save lots of costs from this deployment. The considerations here would be how much work this company is doing can benefit from AI agents? Better yet, What company will benefit if my category of jobs gets replaced? If you are very certain that your job will be replaced in your company, see if your company will benefit in this kind of situation and invest in them, this will be the most direct hedge for losing your job to AI. But investing in your own company entails risk of its own. If your company is on a downtrend or goes bankrupt, both your salaries and investments are gone. Do a bit of research on the essence of the stock market and the companies that will benefit from this kind of situation, then allocate your assets according to that.


The days of the majority of humans getting replaced may still be far away, but rest assured that it is indeed gradually coming to reality. Some jobs will be easier to replace and get replaced first, then as the AI models keep getting better and better, more will lose their income. This is not to be pessimistic and negative about the future. No, I’m quite thrilled about it actually. This is a warning for what is to come, based on observations of technological history and enterprise decision making. The goal of this article is to convince the reader to accept the possibility of losing their jobs to AI and prepare accordingly. The internet and mobile both started mainly around the 2000s, and twenty years later, look at where we are, how fast we progressed. My view is that technology will only progress faster with all the prior technological advancements we made. I wish you the best and hey, thanks for reading.
